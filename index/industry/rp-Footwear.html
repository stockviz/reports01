How have the challenges and oppurtunities evolved over time?

asof: 2026-04-15

The footwear and retail industry has undergone a significant transformation over the years, navigating a complex landscape of regulatory changes, shifting consumer behaviors, and supply chain disruptions. By analyzing the experiences of major players in the Indian market, we can see how historical challenges have been addressed and how these have paved the way for new, structural opportunities.

The Evolution of Challenges Over Time

1. Regulatory Hurdles and the Impact of Taxation One of the most persistent challenges over the last few years has been the changing tax regime. In January 2022, the GST on footwear was increased from 5% to 12%, which crippled organized players like Relaxo Footwears [1-3]. This tax hike created a massive pricing disparity, allowing unorganized and local players to manipulate taxes and capture market share at the lower end of the market [3-5].

While the recent rollout of “GST 2.0” has reduced the rate back to 5% for footwear priced below INR 2,500, this transition has created a new, short-term challenge: channel destocking [6-8]. Distributors currently hold older inventory purchased at the 12% GST rate and are actively reducing primary purchases to liquidate this high-MRP stock before buying new inventory, leading to temporary top-line pressures [8-11]. Furthermore, companies are closely monitoring an “inverted duty structure” where outward duty is 5% but imported inputs remain at 18%, causing temporary working capital and margin concerns [12, 13]. Additional regulatory burdens have also impacted profitability, such as the implementation of the new Labour Codes, which resulted in increased employee benefit expenses and compliance adjustments for companies like Khadim and Bata [14-17].

2. Supply Chain and Sourcing Disruptions Global and regional supply chain volatility has posed significant hurdles. For instance, RedTape faced severe inventory shortages in the previous year due to the implementation of Bureau of Indian Standards (BIS) regulations and political turmoil in Bangladesh, which heavily impacted their ability to fill stores [18-21]. Similarly, Metro Brands had to moderate the rollout of its Foot Locker stores due to BIS-related supply challenges faced by select external brands [22, 23].

3. Shifting Demographics and Brand Relevance Heritage brands have had to battle changing consumer perceptions. Bata, for example, noted that while brand recall is exceptionally high among consumers in their 30s and above, awareness and relevance among consumers in their 20s remain quite low [24-27]. To combat this, they have had to shift marketing spending heavily toward digital platforms and social media influencers, while renovating stores to look younger [28-31]. Additionally, unorganized players producing fake or counterfeit products under branded logos have remained a persistent nuisance in certain regional markets [32].

4. Legacy Retail Formats vs. Changing Economics Historically, traditional Multi-Brand Outlets (MBOs) and Large Format Stores constrained growth. RedTape intentionally exited large format stores and traditional distribution models about five years ago because these formats restricted their sales volumes and growth potential [33-36]. Khadim has also struggled with unprofitable legacy stores, forcing them to close over 60 bleeding outlets recently to optimize working capital and mitigate markdown risks [37-42].

The Evolution of Opportunities Over Time

1. The GST Reversal and Regaining Market Share While the GST reduction caused short-term inventory glitches, it is widely viewed as a massive, long-term structural opportunity. The drop to a 5% GST rate has restored the competitiveness of the organized sector against unorganized players [4, 5, 7, 8, 43]. Companies expect this to dramatically improve affordability in the mass and mid-market segments, leading to volume-led growth and market share recovery [7, 43, 44].

2. Omnichannel Expansion and Digital Transformation The transition from traditional retail to omnichannel models has been one of the most lucrative evolutions. * E-commerce Growth: Online sales have evolved from a supplementary channel to a core growth driver. For Bata, e-commerce has been the fastest-growing channel for the last five years, now contributing to the mid-teens of their business [45-47]. Metro Brands saw its digital sales grow by 35% over a nine-month period, accounting for 13% of its total revenue [48]. * Direct-to-Consumer (D2C): Companies are heavily investing in their proprietary platforms to protect margins. Khadim is shifting focus away from third-party pure-play marketplaces—where heavy discounting leads to losses—to push sales aggressively through its own revamped websites (khadims.com and britishwalkers.com) [49-54].

3. Premiumization and New Product Categories As India’s per capita GDP rises, consumers are shifting toward discretionary spending and premium products [55, 56]. * Sneakers and Athleisure: This segment has seen explosive growth. Campus Activewear doubled its sneaker portfolio year-over-year, leading to a 5.2% increase in its Average Selling Price (ASP) [57]. They also successfully ventured into athleisure apparel to unlock incremental revenue from their existing customer base [58]. * Premium Sub-brands: Companies are leveraging premium sub-brands to drive higher margins. Khadim’s premium brand, British Walkers, saw nearly 10% year-on-year growth, and its partnership with Skechers doubled its sales sequentially [59-62]. RedTape has also moved aggressively into premium categories by introducing ETPU soles over basic EVA, launching premium leather collections, and debuting an outdoor trekking brand, “Ozark,” which commands higher price points [63-66].

4. Asset-Light Network Expansion To penetrate Tier 2, Tier 3, and suburban markets efficiently, companies have evolved their expansion strategies to favor asset-light franchise models. Bata historically struggled with the franchise model but has successfully scaled it from under 50 stores a few years ago to nearly 2,000 locations today [67, 68]. Khadim is adopting a similar asset-light strategy, deciding to expand primarily through franchises while only opening company-owned stores in highly strategic, profitable locations [69, 70].

5. Manufacturing and Sourcing Efficiencies Operational strategies have evolved to maximize agility and cost-effectiveness. Bata is systematically reducing its reliance on in-house manufacturing (scaling down from 35% to mid-teens) to leverage the technology and faster lead times of consolidated contract manufacturing partners [71-74]. Conversely, companies like Campus Activewear have stabilized their dedicated in-house manufacturing facilities to secure their supply of premium sneaker uppers and swiftly meet rising demand [58, 75]. Additionally, new Free Trade Agreements (FTAs) are opening up vast opportunities for companies to scale up their export businesses to global markets [76, 77].

What are the headwinds affecting this industry?

asof: 2026-04-15

The footwear industry is currently navigating a challenging demand environment characterized by muted consumer sentiment [1, 2]. There is a notable softness in the mass market and value-driven segments driven by continued pressure on discretionary spending [3, 4]. Consumers are exhibiting hesitancy, which has led to overall measured consumption trends and subdued market conditions [4-6].

Regulatory and Compliance Costs The implementation of the new Labour Codes and recent government norms has significantly increased employee-related costs and compliance adjustments [7, 8]. Multiple companies in the sources reported facing one-time costs and higher employee benefit expenses stemming from these unified labour regulations, which have negatively impacted their profitability and margins [6, 9-11]. Additionally, the enforcement of Bureau of Indian Standards (BIS) regulations has created supply challenges and troubled inventory pipelines for the industry [12-14].

Taxation Hurdles and Channel Destocking The announcement and rollout of the GST 2.0 framework caused significant disruptions, including delayed purchases and widespread channel destocking [3, 15]. Distributors and retailers prioritized liquidating their old, higher-priced inventory before buying new stock, as they were worried about how to adjust their GST inputs [16, 17]. Furthermore, the industry is grappling with an inverted duty structure, where the outward tax rate on footwear was reduced to 5%, but import duties on essential raw materials remain high at 18% [18].

Intense Competition from Unorganized and Counterfeit Players The industry faces fierce competition from a highly fragmented market and an influx of unbranded, local players [19, 20]. Previously, a tax difference allowed these smaller, non-tax-paying local players to offer higher margins to retailers and manipulate pricing, negatively affecting the sales of organized brands [21, 22]. Additionally, there is an ongoing issue with fake products and copies of branded logos being manufactured in huge quantities, particularly in Northern markets, which act as direct competition at lower price points [23, 24].

Supply Chain and Geopolitical Disruptions External macro events, such as the turmoil in Bangladesh, have taken a heavy toll on the industry’s supply chain [12, 13]. These disruptions caused severe stock shortages during critical periods, negatively impacting sales because companies did not have enough inventory to fill their stores [12, 13]. On a global scale, weak economic conditions and an unsustainable cost of living in Western countries are creating economic headwinds that make international export markets less attractive for expansion [25, 26].

Seasonal and Margin Pressures Climatic factors have also played an unexpected role; for instance, the absence of a proper winter season in North India negatively impacted the sales of autumn and winter footwear collections [27, 28]. To combat these multifaceted challenges, retain market share against unorganized competition, and support their distributors, companies have been forced to rely heavily on increased sales promotions and deeper discounting, which has ultimately put downward pressure on their gross and EBITDA margins [9, 29].

What are the key things to understand about this industry?

asof: 2026-04-15

The Indian footwear industry is currently undergoing a significant transformation driven by regulatory changes, evolving consumer demographics, and strategic shifts in retail and manufacturing. Based on the recent communications from major players like Bata, Relaxo, RedTape, Khadim, and Campus Activewear, here are the key things to understand about the industry:

1. The Impact of GST 2.0 and Regulatory Tailwinds A major structural change in the industry is the implementation of “GST 2.0”, which reduced the tax rate on footwear priced below INR 2,500 to 5% (down from 12% or 18%) [1-3]. Since a vast majority of the market’s portfolio falls under this price bracket (e.g., 98% of Relaxo’s and 99% of RedTape’s products), this tax reduction has substantially improved affordability and leveled the playing field for organized players against the unorganized sector [1-4]. Additionally, the government’s implementation of the Bureau of Indian Standards (BIS) Quality Control Orders has helped restrict cheap Chinese imports, further benefiting domestic manufacturers [5, 6].

2. Premiumization and Increasing Average Selling Prices (ASP) Consumers are increasingly shifting toward premium footwear, and companies are adjusting their portfolios to capture higher margins [7, 8]. * RedTape is launching premium outdoor brands like Ozark and premium leather collections, which has helped push their ASP up by 8% to 10% [9, 10]. * Campus Activewear reported a 5.2% YoY increase in ASP driven by premiumization [11]. * Khadim is witnessing double-digit growth in its premium sub-brands (like British Walkers and Sharon), noting that these premium lines command higher margins and are expected to eventually make up 20% to 25% of their retail mix [12-14].

3. The Boom in Sneakers and Athleisure Driven by consumers prioritizing health, wellness, and self-expression, the demand for sports shoes, sneakers, and athleisure is soaring [15]. * Campus Activewear saw its sneaker portfolio nearly double year-over-year and has strategically ventured into athleisure apparel to capture incremental revenue [11, 16]. * Bata acknowledges that sneakers heavily dominate the consideration of younger demographics, leading them to create “Sneaker Studios” and introduce younger-looking store formats [17, 18]. * RedTape is innovating in this space by moving away from basic EVA soles to advanced ETPU materials for better comfort and performance [9, 19].

4. Aggressive Expansion in Tier 2/3 Markets via Franchise Models To reach a wider audience, especially in rapid-growth suburban and Tier 2/Tier 3 markets, organized brands are heavily relying on an asset-light franchise model [20, 21]. * Bata has expanded its franchise network to nearly 2,000 stores, recognizing it as an efficient, highly profitable way to tap into smaller consumer cohorts [20-22]. * RedTape operates over 500 stores, primarily focusing on 80-100 new store openings per year in Tier 2 and 3 cities across South, West, and East India [23-25]. * Khadim is moving towards a largely asset-light model, relying on franchisees for future expansions while maintaining company-owned stores only in strategic, highly profitable locations [26, 27].

5. E-Commerce and Digital Transformation Digital channels are no longer just an afterthought but a core growth driver. * RedTape garners 30% of its business from online platforms and is currently focusing on driving traffic to its own Direct-to-Consumer (D2C) websites to improve profitability over third-party marketplaces [28-31]. * Campus Activewear runs a robust online-to-offline capability, boasting a 34.6% CAGR in e-commerce revenue over recent years [32]. * Bata has seen its e-commerce contribution reach the mid-double digits, with 14% of its D2C business coming through its newly launched app within just six months [22, 33].

6. Contrasting Manufacturing Strategies and Inventory Optimization Companies are taking different approaches to backend operations: * In-house vs. Outsourced: While Relaxo heavily highlights its massive in-house manufacturing capacity (10.5 lakh pairs per day) and lean manufacturing techniques [34, 35], Bata is actively reducing its in-house manufacturing (from 30-35% down to mid-teens) to focus on a consolidated list of highly capable contract manufacturers [36, 37]. RedTape relies on contract manufacturing with massive minimum order quantities (15,000+ pairs per color) to negotiate the best possible pricing [38, 39]. * Inventory Efficiency: Across the board, brands are working to declutter and increase inventory freshness. Bata attributes recent margin improvements to its Zero Base Merchandising (ZBM) project and deep inventory rationalization [40-42]. Khadim consciously reduced purchases to optimize its working capital and lower its inventory days [43, 44].

7. Hyper-Targeting Gen Z and “Discount Culture” The Indian consumer base is young, and brands are tailoring their marketing and pricing strategies accordingly. Bata is shifting its marketing investments from traditional celebrities to social media influencers to capture consumers in their 20s [17, 18]. Meanwhile, RedTape has built a highly successful business model around Indian consumers’ love for deals; they introduce 50 new styles a month to drive repeat footfall every 60 days, and they strategically operate on a permanent “70% off” discount model, which has proven incredibly popular with Gen Z shoppers [45-48].

What are the tailwinds affecting this industry?

asof: 2026-04-15

The footwear industry is currently benefiting from several structural, regulatory, and consumer-driven tailwinds that are accelerating growth and profitability for organized players.

1. GST Rationalization (GST 2.0) A significant regulatory tailwind across the industry is the recent implementation of GST 2.0, which reduced the tax rate to 5% for footwear priced below INR 2,500 [1, 2]. This has served as a massive catalyst by: * Improving Competitiveness: The tax cut has leveled the playing field, making organized footwear brands much more competitive against the unorganized sector, which historically benefited from tax arbitrage [1, 3, 4]. * Boosting Affordability and Demand: The reduced tax burden has improved affordability in the mass and mid-market segments [1]. Companies like Bata, Relaxo, RedTape, Metro Brands, and Campus Activewear all explicitly cited this GST reduction as a driver for positive sales momentum, increased consumer footfalls, and volume recovery [5-9]. RedTape, for instance, noted that offering deeper discounts post-GST reduction led to an uptick in repeat customers and higher purchase volumes [7, 10].

2. Premiumization There is a distinct consumer shift towards premium footwear, allowing companies to improve their Average Selling Price (ASP) and profit margins. * Bata reported robust growth driven disproportionately by its premium brands like Hush Puppies, Power, and Floatz [11-13]. * Khadim experienced a nearly 10% year-over-year growth in premium sub-brands like British Walkers and Sharon, which carry higher margins than their base portfolio [14-17]. * Campus Activewear saw its ASP rise by 5.2% year-over-year, aided by a higher salience of premium SKUs and strong acceptance of refreshed collections [18]. * RedTape is actively elevating its ASP by launching premium product lines like the outdoor brand Ozark and premium leather collections [19, 20].

3. Surging Demand for Sneakers and Athleisure Consumers are increasingly prioritizing health, wellness, and comfort, leading to a massive shift toward specially designed sports shoes, sneakers, and athleisure wear [21]. * Campus Activewear noted that its sneaker portfolio almost doubled compared to the previous year [18]. * Bata management highlighted that the sneaker space is highly dominant in the consideration set for younger consumers in their 20s [22, 23]. * Relaxo and Khadim are also strategically focusing on launching and expanding their presence in the sneaker and athleisure categories to capture this trend [24-26].

4. Digital Transformation and E-Commerce Growth India’s digitally savvy population and the rise of online shopping, virtual try-ons, and quick commerce platforms are creating new avenues for rapid growth [21, 27]. * Metro Brands reported a 24% growth in e-commerce and omni-channel sales during the quarter [9]. * Bata successfully launched its own app, which now accounts for 14% of its Direct-to-Consumer (D2C) business within just six months [28, 29]. * RedTape derives 30% of its business from online channels and is aggressively expanding its warehouse footprint to cater to the fast-growing quick commerce segment [30-32]. Khadim is also actively shifting focus to scale its proprietary websites to secure higher-margin online sales [33, 34].

5. Favorable Demographics and Economic Growth India’s young demography and rising economic prosperity represent a massive long-term tailwind [27, 35]. As per capita GDP surpasses the $2,500 to $2,700 mark, consumers naturally increase their spending on discretionary categories like fashion and footwear [35]. Furthermore, millennial consumer choices—which heavily emphasize personal style and self-expression—are reshaping product demand [21].

6. Formalization of the Footwear Market The organized footwear segment is capturing a larger slice of the pie. Industry projections indicate that the organized market’s share is expected to grow from 35% in 2024 to 45% by 2034, allowing branded players to outgrow the overall industry rate [36].

7. Strong Festive and Wedding Season Demand Shorter-term macroeconomic tailwinds include a robust festive and wedding season, which provided a significant boost to revenue and consumer interaction for brands like Metro Brands, Campus Activewear, and Relaxo during the reported periods [8, 9, 37].

What is the general outlook of this industry?

asof: 2026-04-15

The footwear industry is currently transitioning from a period of sluggish demand into a highly optimistic recovery phase, driven by favorable tax reforms, shifting consumer preferences toward premiumization, and strong macroeconomic tailwinds.

Recent Challenges and Subdued Demand In recent quarters, the industry has navigated a challenging environment characterized by muted consumer sentiment and continued pressure on discretionary spending [1, 2]. This softness was particularly pronounced in the mass market and value-driven segments, which negatively impacted the top-line growth of several organized players serving middle- and lower-income demographics [2-4]. While urban markets demonstrated pockets of resilience during festive periods, overall consumption trends remained measured, forcing companies to rely on disciplined cost control and prudent inventory management to protect their margins [2, 5].

The “GST 2.0” Catalyst Despite these recent hurdles, the general outlook for the industry has turned highly positive, largely due to the government’s implementation of the new GST framework [3, 6, 7]. The reduction of the GST rate to 5% for footwear priced below INR 2,500 (down from previous rates of 12% and 18%) is acting as a massive structural tailwind [8-10].

This tax rationalization is reshaping the industry outlook in several ways: * Improved Competitiveness: The lower tax rate has leveled the playing field, making organized footwear brands significantly more competitive against unorganized and local players who previously thrived on tax manipulation [8, 9, 11, 12]. * Enhanced Affordability: The price reduction has improved affordability for consumers in the mass and mid-market segments, which is expected to spur volume-led growth [6, 8, 13]. * Gradual Revival: While it has taken some time for distributors and retailers to liquidate their older, higher-priced inventory, companies are already witnessing a gradual revival in demand and footfalls [3, 14, 15]. The full positive impact of this GST reduction is expected to materialize strongly in the upcoming quarters and into the next fiscal year [16, 17].

Shift Towards Premiumization, Sneakers, and Athleisure Another major factor driving the industry’s growth outlook is a robust consumer shift toward premium products, sneakers, and athleisure [18-20]. Consumers are increasingly prioritizing health, wellness, personal style, and self-expression, leading to higher demand for specially designed, comfortable sports shoes [21].

To capitalize on this, footwear companies are aggressively innovating and expanding their portfolios: * Higher Average Selling Prices (ASP): Brands are launching premium sub-brands, outdoor/trekking shoes, and premium leather collections, which is successfully driving up their ASPs and lifting blended profit margins [18, 20, 22, 23]. * Dominance of Sneakers: The sneaker segment is dominating the consideration of younger demographics (Gen Z and millennials), with companies reporting that their sneaker portfolios have nearly doubled year-over-year [20, 24]. Premium brands and performance footwear within company portfolios are driving growth disproportionately better than basic open footwear [25].

Macro-Economic and Demographic Tailwinds Over the long term, the Indian footwear industry is positioned on the cusp of a major consumption boom [26]. As the country’s per capita GDP increases beyond the INR 2,500 to INR 2,700 mark, consumers naturally shift toward higher discretionary spending [26]. Furthermore, India’s exceptionally young demographic profile, combined with rapid digital transformation, presents a massive runway for growth [21, 27]. Companies are actively leveraging e-commerce, quick commerce platforms, and omnichannel strategies to reach these digital-savvy consumers [27-29]. Additionally, brands are aggressively expanding their physical retail footprints, specifically targeting high-potential Tier 2 and Tier 3 markets through asset-light franchise models [30, 31].

Conclusion While the industry is just emerging from a period of persistent top-line weakness, the outlook for the upcoming quarters and FY27 is exceptionally optimistic [32-34]. Supported by robust festive demand, the undeniable benefits of GST rationalization, and a strategic pivot toward high-margin premium and athleisure categories, organized footwear companies expect a strong recovery trajectory, with many anticipating a return to steady double-digit growth [6, 28, 33, 35].
