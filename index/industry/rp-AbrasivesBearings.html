How have the challenges and oppurtunities evolved over time?

asof: 2026-04-16

Opportunities have increasingly been driven by favorable global trade realignments, targeted domestic infrastructure spending, and strategic capacity expansions.

  • Favorable Trade Agreements and Policy Shifts: Recent global trade developments, particularly the ongoing trade engagements and agreements between India, the United States, and the European Union, are expected to create highly favorable opportunities for Indian manufacturing [1, 2]. These deeper trade partnerships and the push by global OEMs to diversify supply chains are strengthening export opportunities for industrial products over the medium to long term [1, 2]. For instance, a reduction in US tariffs from 50% to 18% is anticipated to significantly benefit Indian exporters [3, 4]. Similarly, Free Trade Agreements (FTAs) with the EU are projected to lower tariff rates by 4% to 5% compared to standard Most Favored Nation (MFN) categories, tangibly increasing the competitiveness of Indian companies [5, 6]. Furthermore, policy shifts in rival nations, such as China removing its 9% export rebate on abrasive products, are actively helping Indian manufacturers gain domestic market share [7, 8].
  • Sectoral Growth and Infrastructure Allocations: Domestic demand is being bolstered by sustained government capital allocations toward rail infrastructure and wagon building, which guarantees a steady growth trajectory for related industrial suppliers [9, 10]. Simultaneously, the commercial vehicle segment (both on-highway and off-highway) has shown an accelerated pull for industrial components like bearings [11-13]. Growth is also surging in advanced, new-age applications; for example, industrial ceramics utilized in Solid Oxide Fuel Cells (SOFCs) are experiencing growth rates north of 20%, resulting in record-high order books [14-16].
  • Strategic Restructuring and Capacity Expansion: To capture these opportunities, companies are heavily investing in localized capacities and operational agility. Significant greenfield investments have been made to substitute imports and boost exports, such as new roller bearing plants in Bharuch and railway portfolio expansions in Jamshedpur [17-19]. Others are doubling manufacturing capacities for cutting and grinding wheels to serve the booming fabrication and construction sectors [20, 21]. Additionally, corporate restructuring, such as the demerger of SKF into a focused industrial entity, has allowed for sharper market orientation, faster decision-making, and the establishment of new state-of-the-art facilities to support India’s infrastructure build-out [22, 23]. Companies are also optimizing operating costs by investing in Special Purpose Vehicles (SPVs) for sourcing renewable solar energy [24, 25].

Conversely, challenges have evolved to include severe geopolitical disruptions, aggressive global competition, macroeconomic volatility, and transitional regulatory hurdles.

  • Geopolitical Disruptions and Sanctions: The volatile geopolitical situation has directly hindered export offtake to regions like Eastern Europe, the UK, and Southeast Asia [26]. Certain overseas subsidiaries have been drastically impacted by international conflicts; for example, operations located in Russia came under US sanctions, leading to year-over-year sales drops of 36% to 46% [27-29]. Additionally, uncertainties surrounding shifting US import tariffs have caused end-customers to defer or delay large industrial projects, leading to sluggishness in certain export segments [30, 31].
  • Intensified Competition and Macroeconomic Pressures: Indian subsidiaries operating abroad have faced severe structural market challenges. Global overcapacity and intense price pressure from Chinese competitors have severely eroded realizations [32, 33]. This is often compounded by adverse currency fluctuations, such as the strong appreciation of the South African Rand, which has wiped out the benefits of increased sales volumes and resulted in net losses [32-34]. Macroeconomic burdens, particularly high energy prices and elevated wage and labor costs in Europe, have proven insurmountable for some entities, ultimately forcing parent companies to initiate voluntary winding-up processes for long-underperforming international subsidiaries [35, 36].
  • Regulatory Changes and Transitional Costs: Domestically, the transition to the new Government of India Labour Codes—which consolidated existing labor laws—has required companies to recognize significant incremental obligations and provisions for gratuity due to changes in the definition of wages, temporarily compressing profit margins [37-40]. Operational transitions also present short-term hurdles; companies have had to absorb high initial fixed costs and depreciation impacts (ramp-up costs) while waiting for customer approvals (PPAPs) at newly commissioned plants [41-43]. Restructuring efforts have similarly incurred massive one-time stamp duty, professional, and transfer premium expenses [22, 44]. Finally, companies must continually navigate complex regulatory scrutiny, occasionally facing unexpected tax demands and penalties related to excess Input Tax Credit (ITC) claims or assessment unit calculations, requiring lengthy appeals and rectifications [45, 46].

What are the headwinds affecting this industry?

asof: 2026-04-16

Geopolitical and Macroeconomic Instability A major headwind affecting the industry is the continuing geopolitical crisis and severe macroeconomic challenges, which are significantly impacting global operations and input costs. For instance, Carborundum Universal Limited (CUMI) reported that its Russian subsidiary, Volzhsky Abrasive Works (VAW), experienced a massive 36% to 46% drop in sales due to U.S. sanctions imposed in January 2025 [1-4]. Additionally, Wendt (India) Limited experienced a 12% year-over-year drop in export sales due to a volatile geopolitical situation and lower offtake from countries and regions including Indonesia, the UK, Thailand, and Eastern Europe [5].

Macroeconomic conditions in Europe have also driven up input costs, rendering energy, wages, and labor costs very high for entities like CUMI’s German subsidiary, AWUKO [6]. Currency fluctuations pose another significant financial challenge; CUMI’s South African subsidiary, Foskor Zirconia, faced a strong 6% to 8% appreciation of the South African Rand, which negatively impacted its profit and net sales value despite an increase in sales volume [7-10].

Intensified Competition and Pricing Pressures Companies are facing intense price competition exacerbated by global overcapacity [6]. Foskor Zirconia suffered a 13% drop in realization primarily due to aggressive price pressure from Chinese competitors [7-10]. Similarly, CUMI’s AWUKO subsidiary has been unable to turn around due to structural market challenges, product and market limitations, and intensified price competition in a market with excess capacity [6, 11, 12]. These mounting losses and the inability to recover ultimately led CUMI to initiate a voluntary winding-up process for the German entity [11, 12].

Weak Demand and Project Delays Certain segments are suffering from sluggish demand and deferred capital investments. CUMI noted that its wear ceramics business has faced growth challenges because end customers in the U.S. have delayed or deferred projects over the last two quarters [13, 14]. This hesitation is largely driven by uncertainty regarding import costs and tariffs, causing customers to wait for clarity before proceeding with their investments [13, 14]. Additionally, CUMI’s project-based fire refractory business experienced muted growth due to the “bunching” of products [15, 16].

Operational and Transitional Cost Burdens Several companies are grappling with margin compression due to operational transitions, unfavorable product mixes, and the under-recovery of fixed costs: * Under-recovery of fixed costs: CUMI AWUKO incurred higher losses because it halted production for a period to optimize piling inventory, which completely eliminated its ability to absorb and recover fixed costs [17-20]. * Logistical disruptions: CUMI’s subsidiary, Rhodius, lost close to EUR 5 million in sales in a single quarter simply due to the logistical disruption of shifting its warehouses [21, 22]. * Plant ramp-up costs: Timken India Limited experienced margin compression (a 170 basis point impact on profit before tax) due to significant ramp-up costs and incremental depreciation (approximately INR 9 to 10 crores a quarter) from its newly capitalized Bharuch plant, which is currently operating at a lower capacity utilization of around 30% [23-26]. * Unfavorable product mix: Timken also cited an unfavorable sales mix—specifically lower sales in its highly profitable rail segment compared to previous quarters—which negatively impacted overall gross margins [27-30]. * Restructuring expenses: SKF India (Industrial) Limited faced significant one-time exceptional items related to a corporate demerger, including IT costs, professional services, stamp duty, and estimated transfer premiums payable to statutory authorities, aggregating to over Rs. 1,800 million [31, 32].

Regulatory, Tariff, and Statutory Compliance Costs The industry is bearing the financial burden of new regulatory requirements, trade tariffs, and tax disputes. A widespread headwind across the Indian manufacturing sector is the implementation of the New Labour Codes by the Government of India (effective November 21, 2025), which consolidates existing labor laws and changes wage definitions [33, 34]. This has forced companies to recognize estimated incremental obligations and provisions towards past service costs on employee gratuity and compensated absences: * SKF India recognized an incremental impact of Rs. 34 million on gratuity primarily due to the change in wage definitions [34]. * Grindwell Norton recognized an estimated incremental obligation on gratuity amounting to Rs. 100.27 lakhs [33]. * Wendt (India) Limited recognized a provision of Rs. 19 lakhs towards past service costs [35]. * Timken India noted a 60-basis point transitional impact on its profit before tax due to these new Labor Codes [25, 26].

Furthermore, regulatory scrutiny in the form of tax disputes is also acting as a headwind; for example, Grindwell Norton received an order from the CGST and Central Excise authority demanding payment of tax and penalties aggregating to over Rs. 5.36 lakhs relating to alleged excess Input Tax Credit (ITC) claims [36, 37]. High import tariffs also act as a constraint on supply chains, with Timken noting that industrial bearings imported from the USA attract a duty of around 8% plus cess, while U.S. tariffs on some inbound products had previously been raised as high as 50% [38-41].

What are the key things to understand about this industry?

asof: 2026-04-16

The industrial manufacturing sector—encompassing bearings, abrasives, technical ceramics, and electrominerals—is currently undergoing significant transformation driven by shifting global trade dynamics, targeted capacity expansions, and evolving end-user demand.

Here are the key things to understand about the industry based on the provided financial and operational disclosures:

1. Favorable Global Trade Shifts and Supply Chain Diversification Global OEMs are increasingly focusing on supply chain diversification and higher localization, positioning India as a preferred manufacturing destination [1]. The industry is highly sensitive to international trade agreements: * Tariff Reductions: Ongoing trade engagements between India, the United States, and the European Union are expected to create strong export opportunities [1, 2]. For instance, headline tariffs in the US for certain engineered goods are anticipated to drop from 50% to 18% [3, 4]. Similarly, Free Trade Agreements (FTAs) with the EU are expected to lower tariffs by 4% to 5%, significantly improving the competitiveness of Indian manufacturers [5]. * The “China Factor”: The industry faces a dual-edged relationship with China. On one hand, intense price pressure and global overcapacity driven by Chinese competition continue to challenge profitability in segments like electrominerals [6, 7]. On the other hand, China’s recent removal of export rebates (dropping from 9% to 0%) on abrasive products like grinding wheels is a major positive catalyst that will help Indian manufacturers capture more domestic market share [8, 9].

2. Geopolitical Volatility and Macroeconomic Pressures While trade deals present upside, geopolitical volatility remains a persistent headwind. Lower offtake from regions like Eastern Europe, the UK, and Southeast Asia has negatively impacted export volumes [10]. Furthermore, geopolitical crises have caused severe supply chain and project delays; for example, uncertainty around US import tariffs has caused customers to defer large ceramic projects [11, 12]. Additionally, high input costs—specifically energy and labor—combined with global overcapacity, have severely impacted the financial viability of certain foreign subsidiaries, even leading to their voluntary winding up [7, 13].

3. Distinct Dynamics Across Key End-User Segments The demand for industrial products varies heavily depending on the specific end-user market: * Railways: The rail business provides slow, steady, and reliable growth rather than sharp “hockey stick” spikes, as it is heavily reliant on government capital allocations and infrastructure budgets [14-17]. Crucially, the rail sector has very high entry barriers because bearings and related parts are critical safety items that require stringent certifications [18, 19]. * Commercial Vehicles (CV) and Automobiles: This segment is witnessing steady acceleration and volume increases in both on-highway and off-highway categories, pulling steady demand for bearings [20-23]. * General Engineering and Construction: There is strong, broad-based growth in fabrication, construction, and general engineering, which serve as the primary consumers for cutting and grinding wheels [24]. * New-Age Technologies: Manufacturers are seeing rapid growth (north of 20%) and record order books for engineered ceramics used in advanced applications, such as Solid Oxide Fuel Cells (SOFCs) [25-27]. There is also expanding opportunity in electric vehicle (EV) infrastructure, such as precision stamping and custom connectors for EV batteries [28].

4. Aggressive Capacity Expansion and Advanced Automation To capitalize on these domestic and export opportunities, companies are aggressively investing in greenfield plants and capacity doubling [29-33]. * State-of-the-Art Manufacturing: New facilities are being equipped with automated press lines, SCADA-based process controls, unmanned oven handling, and ambient control systems to ensure product consistency and cost competitiveness [32, 33]. * Quality Certifications: Meeting global safety standards is paramount; for example, obtaining the Organization for the Safety of Abrasives (oSa) certification is critical for cutting and grinding tools [33, 34]. * Short-Term Margin Impact: It is important to note that the ramp-up costs and depreciation associated with these massive new plant investments can temporarily compress profit margins until optimal capacity utilization (usually targeted above 45-50%) is achieved [35-37].

5. Strategic Restructuring and Sustainability Initiatives To navigate this dynamic landscape, companies are restructuring to become more agile. Corporate demergers are being utilized to create strategically independent, sector-focused entities that can make faster decisions and better support the accelerating infrastructure build-out [38]. Simultaneously, the industry is prioritizing sustainability by embedding digital capabilities and shifting toward renewable energy, such as investing in Special Purpose Vehicles (SPVs) to source solar power for manufacturing operations [38-40].

What are the tailwinds affecting this industry?

asof: 2026-04-16

Global trade developments and deeper trade partnerships are acting as significant tailwinds for the Indian engineering, industrial products, and bearings sectors [1, 2]. Recent trade agreements and ongoing engagements between India, the United States, and the European Union are creating highly favorable opportunities for Indian manufacturing [1]. Specifically, U.S. tariffs on certain engineered goods are anticipated to drop drastically from 50% to 18%, which is expected to improve the cost position and help Indian manufacturers gain market share [3-6]. Similarly, a recently concluded EU Free Trade Agreement (FTA) is expected to lower tariff rates by 4% to 5% compared to the existing Most Favored Nation (MFN) category, which will noticeably increase the competitiveness of Indian exports to Europe [7]. These trade benefits are compounded by a broader macroeconomic trend of global OEMs seeking supply chain diversification and increasing their localization requirements, which firmly positions India as a preferred global manufacturing destination [2].

The industry is also benefiting from favorable regulatory shifts in competing manufacturing hubs. Notably, China recently removed its export rebate on abrasive products, including grinding wheels, reducing it from 9% to 0% [8]. This policy change is viewed as a highly positive development that will significantly help Indian companies strengthen their domestic market position and compete much more effectively against Chinese imports over time [8].

Furthermore, there is robust and accelerating demand across several core domestic and industrial sectors: * Commercial Vehicles: The commercial vehicle segment, encompassing both on-highway and off-highway vehicles, is experiencing steady volume growth and acceleration, which is directly pulling more demand for industrial bearings [9-11]. Original Equipment Manufacturers (OEs) in this space are continuing to build up stock and drive sales [12]. * Rail Infrastructure: The Indian government’s recent budget has improved capital allocation for the rail sector [13]. This continued government spending ensures that infrastructure development around the rail ecosystem will be sustained, providing a steady, long-term pipeline of demand for railway bearings and components [13-15]. * Construction and General Engineering: Key consumer industries for industrial consumables like cutting and grinding wheels—such as fabrication, construction, and general engineering—are witnessing strong growth [16]. To capitalize on this high demand, companies are investing heavily in capacity additions, including state-of-the-art automated manufacturing facilities [16, 17].

Finally, the transition toward new-age technologies is creating fresh avenues for high-margin growth. Within the advanced ceramics segment, businesses supplying components for Solid Oxide Fuel Cells (SOFCs) and other high-end applications are experiencing robust growth of over 20% and are securing record-high order books [18, 19]. Additionally, new alternative materials, such as Fiber Reinforced Composites (FRC), are being actively developed and manufactured to cater to emerging applications, including the Electric Vehicle (EV) market and specialized process industries [20, 21].

What is the general outlook of this industry?

asof: 2026-04-16

The general outlook for the industrial products, bearings, abrasives, and ceramics manufacturing industry is broadly optimistic, driven by favorable global trade developments and strong domestic demand in key end-user segments, despite facing certain geopolitical and macroeconomic headwinds.

Favorable Global Trade Dynamics and Export Opportunities A major driver for the positive outlook is the evolving global trade landscape. Progress towards deeper trade partnerships, particularly the ongoing trade engagements between India, the United States, and the European Union, is expected to create highly favorable opportunities for Indian manufacturing [1, 2]. These developments are supporting supply chain diversification by global Original Equipment Manufacturers (OEMs) and are anticipated to significantly strengthen export opportunities for engineering and industrial product companies in the medium to long term [3, 4]. Furthermore, China’s recent decision to remove its export rebate on abrasive products (dropping it from 9% to 0%) is viewed as a positive catalyst that will enhance the competitiveness and market position of Indian manufacturers [5, 6]. Free Trade Agreements (FTAs) are also expected to lower tariff rates by 4% to 5%, further increasing the industry’s global competitiveness [7, 8].

Sector-Specific Demand Drivers The industry is witnessing robust demand across several critical infrastructure and industrial sectors: * Commercial Vehicles and Automotive: There is a notable acceleration in the commercial vehicle segment, encompassing both on-highway and off-highway applications, driving higher volumes of component sales [9-12]. Domestic sales are also benefiting from increased demand from auto ancillaries [13]. * Railways and Infrastructure: The outlook for the rail business is characterized by a “slow and steady” growth trajectory rather than sudden spikes [14, 15]. Recent government budget allocations for rail infrastructure are growing, which ensures that the ecosystem around the rail sector will sustain and expand over the medium to long term, directly benefiting manufacturers of bearings and related safety components [16-19]. * Construction, Fabrication, and General Engineering: Industries such as fabrication, construction, and general engineering—which are key consumers of cutting and grinding wheels—are witnessing strong growth [20, 21]. The sector is also well-positioned to support accelerating infrastructure build-outs, renewables, cement, and heavy engineering [22].

Strategic Capacity Expansions and Localizations To capitalize on this demand, companies are heavily investing in capacity expansion and localization. For example, Carborundum Universal Limited (CUMI) is doubling its cutting and grinding wheel manufacturing capacity from 45 million to over 90 million wheels per annum to meet the strong demand in construction and fabrication [21, 23]. Similarly, Timken India is actively ramping up its new manufacturing facilities to take advantage of increasing localization requirements and the tailwinds provided by recent trade deals [3, 4, 24, 25].

Geopolitical and Macroeconomic Challenges Despite the strong growth drivers, the industry is navigating several notable challenges: * Tariff Uncertainties and Project Delays: In specific markets like the U.S., end customers have deferred or delayed projects due to uncertainty surrounding import tariffs and potential import costs, which has temporarily caused sluggishness in segments like wear ceramics [26, 27]. * Global Overcapacity and Input Costs: In certain international operations, the industry is grappling with intensified price competition exacerbated by global overcapacity and severe pricing pressure from Chinese competitors [28-30]. Ongoing geopolitical crises and macroeconomic challenges have also driven up input costs—particularly energy and labor—which have severely impacted the viability of some European subsidiaries, leading to voluntary winding-down processes [30, 31]. * Export Volatility: The volatile geopolitical situation has led to lower export offtake in several countries and regions, including the UK, Eastern Europe, Indonesia, and Thailand [13].

Financial Guidance and Near-Term Expectations Financially, the outlook remains stable but cautious in certain pockets. For instance, CUMI has maintained its overall consolidated sales growth guidance at 5.5% to 6.5% for the year, alongside a 4% to 5% growth expectation for abrasives [32, 33]. However, it slightly reduced its sales growth guidance for its ceramics division from 16-18% down to 13-14% due to the aforementioned customer project delays, though it still anticipates a strong fourth quarter based on a healthy order backlog [32-35]. Operating margins are expected to normalize gradually as new manufacturing capacities stabilize and initial plant ramp-up costs moderate [36, 37].
