How have the challenges and oppurtunities evolved over time?

asof: 2026-04-16

Evolution of Industry Challenges

  • Stagnant Demand and Global Trade Barriers: The domestic market demand for tiles has been soft and stuck for an extended period [1-3]. In the international arena, exporters historically faced significant hurdles, including high freight charges, international custom duties, and anti-dumping duties, all of which put severe pressure on pricing [1, 2]. For example, quartz exports experienced sudden disruptions in the past due to US policies, which created excess supply and pressure in the domestic market, forcing companies to sell at lower prices [4-6].
  • Overcapacity and Plant Closures: The Morbi ceramic cluster experienced a severe overcapacity scare that led to industry-wide shutdowns [7, 8]. Out of roughly 1,000 units, approximately 300 manufacturing units were forced to close over the last five years due to mounting domestic and export market pressures [7-10]. Furthermore, new town planning and development restrictions have hindered the rapid development of new plants in the region [9-12].
  • Geopolitical and Energy Shocks: Global conflicts have directly impacted operational stability and supply chains. Somany Ceramics, for instance, faced restricted gas supplies—capped at 80% of their past average consumption—due to the Middle East conflict’s impact on global energy markets, which threatened to partially impact production activities [13, 14].
  • High Operating Costs in Retail: High running expenses have made operating international showrooms difficult. This prompted companies like Kajaria Ceramics to close their international retail operations (such as their showrooms in the UAE and the UK) due to the inability to achieve profitability in the short to medium term [15].
  • Severe Industrial Disruptions: Specific companies faced extreme internal crises. Regency Ceramics suffered from unprecedented industrial violence in 2012, resulting in a prolonged lock-out, the destruction of factory assets and equipment, and massive accumulated losses that severely eroded the company’s net worth [16-19].

Evolution of Industry Opportunities

  • Favorable Export Tariffs and Rebounding Markets: The export environment has recently transformed into a major opportunity. Following trade adjustments, the US imposed a 34% duty on Chinese tile exports, compared to an overall duty of just 18% on Indian exports [20-23]. This disparity has given Indian manufacturers a massive competitive advantage globally, allowing quartz exports to successfully resume and driving expectations of strong future growth in large format and quartz exports [4-6, 24-26].
  • Shift to Value-Added and Premium Products: To combat stagnation, companies have evolved by transitioning from traditional small-sized manufacturing to producing premium, value-added lifestyle products. Asian Granito invested heavily in double digital printing technology, robotic designs for quartz, and large-format continuous slab plants, which significantly increased their per-square-meter realization and overall revenue [27-32]. Similarly, Kajaria Ceramics converted a ceramic floor tile unit to Glazed Vitrified Tiles (GVT) to align with prevailing market demands for higher-value products [33].
  • Strategic International Expansion: To bypass freight and duty challenges, some companies have shifted strategies from standard exporting to establishing strong, direct retail bases abroad. Asian Granito has successfully set up stockyards, warehouses, and showrooms in international locations like London, Dubai, Sharjah, Senegal, and Indonesia [34, 35]. Having ready stock available locally has created a turnaround story by eliminating pricing pressures and facilitating immediate sales [34, 35].
  • Reduction in Energy Costs: The introduction of alternative gas suppliers, such as propane gas competing with Sabarmati gas, has created a discount in energy costs [36-39]. Alongside lower international summer gas prices, this has reduced overall production costs, positioning Indian products to capture more of the international market [40-43].
  • Strategic Turnarounds and Favorable Market Conditions: Companies are executing successful strategic turnarounds. NITCO reported a strong 85% month-on-month sales growth driven by higher customer demand, an improved product portfolio, and favorable market conditions [44-46]. Regency Ceramics, despite its historical devastation, is actively refurbishing its Yanam plant to recommence operations and has already started generating revenue and cash flows through a new exclusive manufacturing arrangement for glazed vitrified tiles in Andhra Pradesh [17, 47].

What are the headwinds affecting this industry?

asof: 2026-04-16

Soft Market Demand and Stagnation The ceramics and tile industry has been grappling with a sluggish domestic market that has remained “stuck” for a prolonged period [1, 2]. Overall market demand continues to be notably soft, which has created a challenging environment for manufacturers [3]. This stagnation has put downward pressure on sales realizations, forcing some companies to offer discounts to customers simply to clear out inventory and reduce their stock-keeping units (SKUs) [4].

Geopolitical Conflicts and Energy Supply Restrictions The ongoing conflict in the Middle East has impacted global energy markets, creating significant operational headwinds for the industry [5]. In response to these disruptions, revised supply regulations have been implemented by authorities like the Ministry of Petroleum & Natural Gas, restricting gas supplies to industrial consumers [5]. Manufacturers are now required to maintain their gas consumption at 80% of their past six months’ average, and any overdrawl is invoiced at applicable contractual terms, threatening production capacities and operational costs [5, 6].

Export Disruptions and Domestic Oversupply Slumps in the international export market have sparked industry-wide overcapacity scares [7, 8]. For example, a sudden disruption in quartz exports to the United States caused a severe supply glut in India [9, 10]. This resulted in immense pricing pressure within the domestic market, as manufacturers were forced to sell their excess inventory at significantly lower prices [9-11].

Global Trade Barriers and Freight Costs Companies attempting to offset domestic stagnation by expanding internationally face strict global trade headwinds. Exporters must navigate a complex landscape of international custom duties, anti-dumping duties, and high freight charges [1, 2]. These international barriers consistently exert downward pressure on product pricing and profit margins in various global markets [1, 2].

Widespread Plant Shutdowns and Regulatory Restrictions The combined pressures of the domestic slump and export challenges have led to severe industry-wide shutdowns, particularly in major manufacturing hubs like Morbi [7, 8]. Out of approximately 1,000 units in the region, an estimated 300 units—especially smaller production and glazed vitrified tile (GVT) plants—have been forced to close over the past five years [7, 8]. Furthermore, new government regulations regarding town planning and development in Morbi have imposed spatial restrictions on manufacturers, actively slowing down the previously rapid pace of new plant development [12, 13].

What are the key things to understand about this industry?

asof: 2026-04-16

The ceramics, tiles, and surface solutions industry is undergoing significant transformation driven by changing product mixes, shifting market demands, and evolving operational strategies. Here are the key things to understand about the industry based on the provided company reports and earnings calls:

Product Premiumization and Value Addition Companies are strategically transitioning from traditional, small-sized ceramic floor tiles to value-added products like Glazed Vitrified Tiles (GVT), large-format/big slab tiles, quartz, and composed marble [1], [2], [3]. This shift allows manufacturers to cater to lifestyle, architect-driven, and interior design markets [3]. Moving to larger formats and introducing robotic designs for quartz significantly improves the Average Selling Price (ASP) and per-square-meter realizations, which boosts overall revenue even if volumetric growth remains flat [3], [4], [5]. For instance, large-format products for export can command realizations of ₹408 per square meter compared to ₹265 for domestic products [6]. Additionally, players are expanding into comprehensive home solutions by offering sanitaryware and bathware (faucets) [7], [3], [8].

Export Growth Driven by Favorable Trade Dynamics While the domestic market has faced periods of sluggishness and soft demand, the export market provides a critical growth avenue [9], [10], [11]. Indian tile and quartz manufacturers are expanding their presence in over 100 countries, with key markets including the GCC (Dubai, Sharjah, Abu Dhabi), Europe, Africa, and Southeast Asia [12], [13]. Indian manufacturers are currently benefiting from favorable global trade dynamics, particularly in the US market, where anti-dumping duties on Chinese products (e.g., 34% duty) are notably higher than those levied on Indian products (e.g., 18% duty) [14]. To bypass traditional export margin pressures, companies are increasingly setting up their own international warehouses, stockyards, and showrooms to maintain a strong direct retail base abroad [10], [15].

Shift Towards Retail and Expanding Dealer Networks The industry is pivoting from a predominantly institutional and project-based sales model to a retail-focused approach [16]. To capture the retail market, companies are investing heavily in brand building, onboarding brand ambassadors, and creating exclusive franchisee showrooms [17], [18]. For example, some companies provide financial incentives (like ₹1,500 per square foot) to dealers to build exclusive showrooms with multi-year agreements, ensuring dealer loyalty and better brand visibility [18].

High Inventory Requirements for Retail Models Unlike bulk project sales, which require less inventory holding, a robust retail strategy requires manufacturers to maintain higher inventory levels to provide immediate service [19]. Because retail dealers typically order trucks containing 15 to 20 different items, companies must keep 3 to 4 months of inventory to ensure ready availability of a vast array of designs, sizes, and SKUs [20], [19], [21].

Vulnerability to Energy Costs and Geopolitics Power and fuel, particularly natural gas (such as propane and piped gas), are major operational expenses, constituting around 22% of total production costs [22], [23]. The industry’s margins are highly sensitive to gas price fluctuations; lower international gas prices help reduce production costs and make Indian exports more competitive globally [23], [24]. However, the industry is vulnerable to geopolitical risks. For example, conflicts in the Middle East have previously caused energy market disruptions, leading suppliers like GAIL to restrict industrial gas supplies to 80% of average consumption, forcing plants to evaluate and manage production impacts [25], [26].

Asset-Light Expansion Through Outsourcing To manage capital expenditure (CAPEX) while sustaining growth, major players are increasingly adopting outsourcing models rather than investing heavily in greenfield expansions [27], [28]. Companies leverage their strong brand presence to outsource the manufacturing of standard or project-based tiles, allowing them to focus their in-house CAPEX on digital printing technologies, brand building, and large-format premium tiles [16], [27]. This results in a revenue mix where a substantial portion (e.g., 20% to 30%) comes from traded or outsourced goods [29].

Consolidation in Manufacturing Hubs In primary manufacturing hubs like Morbi in Gujarat, the industry has faced overcapacity scares [30]. Intense domestic and export market pressures have forced hundreds of smaller, traditional tile manufacturing units to shut down in recent years [30]. Furthermore, stringent local town planning regulations are restricting the rapid development of new plants [31]. Consequently, survival and growth in the industry increasingly favor well-capitalized players who can invest in advanced, large-volume technology (such as SACMI Continua machines) and weather the cyclical pressures that force smaller competitors out of the market [17], [30], [31].

What are the tailwinds affecting this industry?

asof: 2026-04-16

Lower Energy and Production Costs A significant tailwind for the industry is the reduction in global gas prices, which is a major cost component for tile manufacturing [1-4]. Furthermore, the introduction of alternative gas supplies, such as propane gas competing against traditional suppliers like Sabarmati gas, has created price competition that has driven costs down even further [5-8]. Because production costs are broadly similar across domestic competitors, this overall drop in energy costs makes Indian manufacturers much more competitive, allowing them to capture a larger share of the international market [2, 4].

Favorable Trade Tariffs and Export Opportunities The industry is benefiting from highly favorable export conditions, particularly due to shifting U.S. trade policies [9-12]. Indian manufacturers are capitalizing on a lower anti-dumping duty of 18%, which is significantly more competitive than the 34% duty imposed on China [13-16]. This tariff advantage is creating massive export opportunities worldwide for Indian companies, particularly for big format tiles and quartz [9, 11, 13, 15]. As a result, manufacturers are successfully expanding their retail and warehouse presence in international markets like the GCC, Europe, Africa, and Southeast Asia to maintain a strong global retail base [17-20].

Shift Toward Premium, Value-Added Products There is a growing consumer preference for premium lifestyle products, architect-based designs, and interior design-focused materials [21, 22]. To meet this demand, companies are shifting their manufacturing focus away from traditional small sizes toward value-added products like Glazed Vitrified Tiles (GVT), large format slabs, and quartz [21-27]. Innovations such as double digital printing and robotic design in quartz are allowing companies to significantly increase their average selling price and realization per square meter, ultimately changing their overall product mix and boosting revenue [21, 22, 28, 29].

Strong Customer Demand and Favorable Market Conditions Broader macroeconomic conditions are providing a supportive environment for the industry. Companies are reporting robust performance driven by higher overall customer demand and generally favorable market conditions [30, 31]. These conditions are allowing companies to strengthen their product portfolios, improve their distribution reach, form strategic partnerships, and successfully execute capacity expansions [30, 31].

What is the general outlook of this industry?

asof: 2026-04-16

The general outlook for the ceramics and tile industry presents a dynamic landscape characterized by soft domestic demand but highly promising export opportunities, structural consolidation, and a strategic shift toward premium products.

Domestic Market and Retail Expansion Currently, the overall domestic market demand has been sluggish and soft [1, 2]. To counter this stagnation, major industry players are pivoting from traditional project-based business models to an aggressive retail-focused strategy [3]. Companies are heavily investing in branding, advertising, and expanding their footprint of exclusive showrooms to strengthen their retail base across India [3-5]. Despite the broader market softness, some companies are experiencing robust recovery, reporting significant month-on-month sales growth driven by higher customer demand and an improved distribution reach [6-8].

Surge in Export Opportunities The international market is acting as a major growth engine for the Indian tile industry. A significant catalyst is the favorable U.S. trade environment; while Chinese tile exports face a heavy 34% duty, Indian exports are subject to only an 18% duty [9]. This positions India as a highly competitive global exporter alongside countries like Turkey, Italy, Spain, and Brazil [9]. To capitalize on this and navigate around international freight charges and anti-dumping duties, companies are establishing their own international warehouses, showrooms, and stockyards in regions like London, Dubai, Senegal, and Indonesia [10]. Industry leaders expect export shares to increase to 18% to 20% of total revenue in the near future, specifically driven by big format tiles and quartz [11].

Consolidation and Outsourcing in Manufacturing The industry is undergoing significant capacity rationalization, particularly in Morbi, Gujarat, which is India’s largest ceramic manufacturing hub. Due to prolonged pressure in both domestic and export markets over the last five years, approximately 300 out of 1,000 manufacturing units in Morbi have shut down, including smaller Glazed Vitrified Tile (GVT) plants [12, 13]. Furthermore, new town planning restrictions in Morbi are limiting the rapid development of new manufacturing facilities [13, 14]. As a result of these closures and restrictions, large players are avoiding heavy capital expenditure (CAPEX) for greenfield ceramic plants; instead, they are shifting toward an outsourcing model where they provide the design and innovation while utilizing existing third-party technology plants for production [15-17].

Product Premiumization To improve profit margins and sales realizations, the industry is transitioning away from manufacturing traditional, small-sized ceramic tiles [18, 19]. Companies are heavily investing in new technologies, such as double digital printing and robotic designs, to manufacture value-added products [19, 20]. The focus is largely on premium offerings like Glazed Vitrified Tiles (GVT), large format slabs, composed marble, and quartz, which command significantly higher per-square-meter realizations [18, 21, 22].

Input Costs and Geopolitical Risks Energy costs are a critical factor for the industry, with gas constituting approximately 22% of total production costs [23, 24]. Increased competition among gas suppliers (such as the availability of propane) has helped lower production costs, allowing Indian manufacturers to price their products more competitively in international markets [25-27]. However, the industry remains vulnerable to global geopolitical events. For example, recent conflicts in the Middle East have disrupted global energy markets, leading authorities like GAIL (India) Limited to restrict natural gas supplies to industrial consumers to 80% of their past six months’ average consumption [28]. Such supply restrictions pose an ongoing operational risk that could impact production capabilities [29].
