The evolution of challenges and opportunities over time is distinctly illustrated across the various companies and sectors detailed in the sources, revealing how structural shifts, market conditions, and strategic adaptations shape business trajectories.

E.I.D. - Parry (India) Limited: Structural Shifts and Strategic Pivots The most striking example of evolving challenges is seen in E.I.D. Parry’s wholly-owned subsidiary, Parry Sugars Refinery India Private Limited (PSRIPL). When the refinery was conceptualized and established in 2006 as an export-oriented unit, the business model was highly commercially viable due to the attractive premiums commanded by white sugar in global markets [1]. The project’s economics were originally premised on the assured availability of natural gas and the lucrative generation of surplus power for the grid [1].

However, over the years, structural shifts materially eroded these foundational opportunities [2]. The non-availability of natural gas forced the company to invest in coal boilers, which significantly increased operating costs [2]. Simultaneously, global white sugar premiums declined sharply, and power export revenues plummeted to just one-third of original projections [2]. These challenges were compounded over time by factory accidents, substantial demurrage charges due to limited vessel frequency on the East Coast, inventory write-offs, and high finance costs [2, 3]. Unfavourable global market conditions and excess refining capacity further eroded profitability, transforming a once-promising opportunity into a structurally unviable business that accumulated losses of Rs. 1,406 Crores, ultimately leading to the decision to close the refinery in March 2026 [2-4].

In its core sugar and biofuels segments, E.I.D. Parry has faced a progressively difficult regulatory and agricultural landscape over the last few years. Costs have continuously risen due to annual increases in the Fair and Remunerative Price (FRP) for sugarcane, while the Minimum Selling Price (MSP) for sugar has seen no upward revision [5, 6]. Furthermore, ethanol offtake prices have remained flat for three years, and there has been an increased market shift toward grain-based ethanol over sugarcane [5]. Agriculturally, lesser cane planting in Tamil Nadu and Andhra Pradesh has resulted in subscale operations, forcing the company to incur higher costs by sourcing molasses to run its distilleries [7].

Despite these compounding challenges, opportunities have evolved through rigorous cost management and operational efficiencies, which have helped the company maintain a better bottom line [7, 8]. E.I.D. Parry is also evolving its business model by expanding its Consumer Products Group (CPG) [6, 9]. While the company faced recent market headwinds from lower pulse prices and had to undertake a conscious restructuring of its distribution channels, it has capitalized on backward integration by setting up its own dal processing plants to ensure quality control and capture better margins [9-13]. Looking to the future, the company is preparing to enter newer food FMCG categories and is actively open to inorganic (M&A) growth opportunities to scale its consumer business [14-16].

Krishival Foods Limited: Scaling Through Turnarounds and Premiumization For Krishival Foods, opportunities have evolved rapidly through aggressive geographic and channel expansion. The company has successfully penetrated deeper into Tier II and Tier III markets, expanding its retail touchpoints to over 10,000 for nuts and 26,000 for ice cream across multiple states [17]. A major evolutionary success has been the turnaround of its “Melt N Mellow” ice cream business, which shifted from a loss to posting positive PAT (Profit After Tax) profitability, driven by improved operational efficiency and capacity utilization despite seasonal softness in the winter [17].

The company is leveraging premiumization, robust festive demand, and GST rate rationalizations to fuel top-line growth and margin expansion [17]. Capitalizing on these opportunities, Krishival recently completed a Rs. 9,999.48 lakh Rights Issue to strengthen its capital base [17]. This funding is being used to drastically evolve its operational scale, expanding its nuts and dried fruits processing capacity from 10 MT to 40 MT per day, and supporting global export ambitions in markets like Singapore and the United States [17].

Orkla India Limited: Digital Transformation Amidst Deflation and Regulatory Scrutiny Orkla India’s evolution highlights the tension between volume growth and pricing challenges. Over time, the company has faced the ongoing challenge of deflation in key raw material prices, which recently caused a 7% negative price impact on its spices portfolio [18, 19]. This deflation meant that while spice volumes grew by a robust 10.1%, revenue only grew by 3.1% [18]. The company has also had to navigate shifts in the festive calendar, which temporarily softened sales in its convenience foods and sweets categories [18]. Additionally, historical regulatory challenges have surfaced over time; the company recently received a GST demand order of over Rs. 2.45 Crores due to the alleged misclassification of “ready to cook” goods spanning from FY 2018-19 to FY 2023-24, though the company intends to contest this [20, 21].

Conversely, opportunities for Orkla have evolved through modernization and international expansion [19]. The company’s digital commerce sector has grown rapidly, surging 43.4% year-over-year [18]. To capture the evolving premium market, Orkla launched “MTR Prakriti,” a digital-first, premium single-source spice brand targeted directly at consumers (D2C) in metropolitan cities [18]. Furthermore, the company is seeing significant evolutionary growth in its international markets, anchored by a 16.4% growth in the GCC region [18, 19].

Manorama Industries Limited: Integration and Capacity Upgrades Manorama Industries has capitalized on the evolving global demand for Cocoa Butter Equivalents (CBE), specialty fats, and exotic butters [22]. The company has seized the opportunity to optimize a newly upgraded fractionation facility, leading to a massive 73.3% year-over-year revenue growth [22]. To address the increasing demand, the company is evolving its infrastructure by debottlenecking its existing facilities to increase capacity by 30% [23]. Furthermore, to secure long-term margin stability and customer loyalty, Manorama has evolved a strategic Rs. 460 Crore capital expenditure plan over the next 2-3 years [24]. This includes setting up new fractionation and refinery facilities, as well as a backward integration processing factory in Burkina Faso, West Africa, to exert unparalleled control over quality, costs, and supply chains [24-27].

Megastar Foods Limited: Navigating Evolving Regulatory Fees Megastar Foods provides an example of how localized regulatory challenges can emerge over time. The company recently received a notice from the Market Committee in Rupnagar alleging non-payment of Market Committee Fees and Rural Development Fund (RDF) fees [28]. While the authority is demanding significant sums, Megastar views this challenge as resolvable, noting that it possesses a valid license from the Punjab State Agricultural Marketing Board until 2030 and maintaining that, under prevalent state laws, processing units in Punjab are exempt from such market fees [29, 30].

What are the headwinds affecting this industry?

The Sugar and Biofuels Industry

The global sugar market is currently transitioning into a surplus environment, bringing significant structural and pricing challenges [1, 2]. A sharp decline in white sugar premiums and excess global refining capacity have materially eroded the fundamental profitability of sugar refiners, substantially reducing their spreads [3, 4]. Furthermore, raw sugar prices have traded lower due to reduced hedge exposure by major Brazilian and Thai millers, coupled with a buildup of short positions by hedge funds that keeps a lid on any price rallies [1]. This is exacerbated by shrinking international demand, as raw sugar requirements from major importers like Indonesia have been revised downwards for multiple consecutive years [5].

In interconnected commodity markets, weak global oil prices are acting as a distinct headwind by keeping ethanol parities at lower levels, which makes the diversion of sugar to ethanol less commercially attractive [5]. The industry is also vulnerable to broad geopolitical risks that can disrupt global trade flows and influence related oil and agricultural markets [3, 5].

On the operational and logistical front, sugar businesses face steep geographic and infrastructural hurdles. Operations situated on India’s East Coast, for instance, are hampered by high freight costs, limited vessel frequencies, and severe constraints in container availability, resulting in an uncompetitive cost structure [4]. Additionally, the non-availability of natural gas has historically forced refiners to invest heavily in alternative coal-based boiler infrastructure, sharply driving up ongoing operating costs and crippling original project economics [3, 6].

Domestically in India, the sugar industry is heavily pressured by agricultural policies. The cost of raw materials continues to climb due to mandatory increases in the Fair and Remunerative Price (FRP) for sugarcane, while the government has provided no clarity on corresponding upward revisions to the Minimum Selling Price (MSP) for refined sugar, effectively squeezing miller margins [7, 8]. The biofuels segment faces similar policy inertia; ethanol offtake prices have remained stagnant for almost three years [7]. Distilleries are also battling an increasing policy and market shift that favors grain-based ethanol over sugarcane-based ethanol [7]. Furthermore, subscale operations caused by reduced cane planting in specific states, such as Tamil Nadu and Andhra Pradesh, force companies to source expensive external molasses just to keep their distilleries running, adding immense cost pressure [9]. Environmental factors compound these agricultural challenges, with El Nino weather patterns threatening crucial rainfall distribution and reservoir levels needed for healthy cane yields [10].

The FMCG, Spices, and Food Processing Industries

The spices and consumer foods segment is currently grappling with persistent deflation in key raw material prices [11, 12]. This deflation creates a negative price impact of approximately 7%, which severely restricts revenue growth even when companies successfully drive higher product sales volumes [11].

Seasonal volatility also dictates performance in this sector. Shifts in the timing of major festivals have been shown to soften demand in specific categories like packaged sweets [11]. Similarly, seasonal weather changes, such as the winter quarter, create inherent demand softness and operational headwinds for the ice cream segment [13].

Finally, unpredictable regulatory and taxation hurdles present substantial obstacles for food processors. Companies have been subjected to sudden and massive financial demands from local authorities, such as claims for alleged non-payment of Market Committee Fees and Rural Development Fund (RDF) fees, even when the processors operate under the legal stance that such fees do not apply to manufacturing and processing units [14-16]. Additionally, businesses face aggressive tax enforcement, including hefty Goods and Services Tax (GST) demands burdened with equivalent interest and penalties, often stemming from complex disputes over the proper classification of “ready to cook” consumer goods [17, 18].

What are the key things to understand about this industry?

asof: 2026-04-15

Government regulations and pricing policies significantly dictate profitability. In the sugar and biofuel sectors, for example, operations are heavily constrained by the Fair and Remunerative Price (FRP) set for sugarcane, the Minimum Selling Prices (MSP) for sugar, and government-fixed ethanol offtake prices [1-3]. Companies often have to rely on policymakers for favorable revisions to these rates and for export quotas to move the needle on profitability [3, 4]. Furthermore, agricultural processing units must navigate state-specific regulations and statutory fees, such as Market Committee Fees and Rural Development Fund (RDF) fees, which require maintaining specific licenses for purchasing, selling, storing, and processing agricultural produce [5, 6].

The industry is highly sensitive to raw material costs and climatic conditions. Input costs fluctuate constantly based on agricultural yields, which are vulnerable to weather patterns like El Nino that can negatively affect rainfall distribution and reservoir levels [7]. Deflation or inflation in commodity prices directly impacts top-line revenue; for instance, a sharp drop in the market price of pulses/lentils or continued deflation in key raw spices can lead to revenue degrowth or stagnation, even when actual sales volumes increase [8-10].

To secure supply chains and capture better margins, companies are increasingly investing in backward and forward integration. On the backward integration front, companies are setting up their own processing plants for raw materials (like dals) to ensure better quality control and cost conversion [11, 12]. Some are even establishing facilities directly at international raw material sources, such as setting up processing factories in Burkina Faso to procure shea nuts and mango kernels [13, 14]. On the forward integration side, companies are heavily investing in capacity expansion—such as upgrading fractionation facilities for specialty fats and cocoa butter equivalents (CBE) or expanding ice cream manufacturing—to meet growing demand and achieve operational leverage [15-17].

Global supply trends, geopolitical factors, and freight logistics heavily influence domestic operations and trade. The industry is deeply impacted by global surpluses or deficits. For example, high sugar outputs from countries like Brazil can keep global raw sugar prices flat and compress “white premiums” (the price spread between raw and refined sugar), making export-oriented refining less lucrative [18-21]. Geographic disadvantages, high freight costs, and constraints in shipping container availability can even render certain export-oriented facilities structurally unviable [22]. Conversely, expanding export reach remains a key growth strategy, with FMCG and specialty fat companies successfully targeting international markets like the GCC, Singapore, and the United States [10, 23-25].

Consumer demand is heavily driven by seasonality, premiumisation, and evolving distribution channels. Sales in categories like nuts, dry fruits, and ice creams are highly seasonal, typically surging during festive and wedding seasons while experiencing softness during winter months [24]. There is a clear consumer shift towards premiumisation, prompting companies to launch premium, single-source products and “digital-first” Direct-to-Consumer (D2C) brands to capture higher value [23, 24]. To deepen market penetration, companies are scaling their digital commerce presence, expanding retail touchpoints into Tier II and Tier III markets, and strengthening cold-chain infrastructure by deploying thousands of deep freezers [23, 24]. Concurrently, businesses are restructuring their traditional distribution channels to focus on more profitable product SKUs and to improve working capital efficiency [8, 26].

What are the tailwinds affecting this industry?

asof: 2026-04-15

The food and fast-moving consumer goods (FMCG) industry is currently benefiting from several positive growth drivers and tailwinds across its various sub-segments, ranging from specialty fats and spices to nuts, dry fruits, and convenience foods.

Improving Macroeconomic and Consumption Trends The industry is experiencing a gradually improving consumption environment supported by strong fundamentals and positive macro tailwinds [1]. Companies are strategically positioned to capitalize on India’s evolving discretionary consumption landscape, which is driving strong volume-led growth [1, 2]. Furthermore, as the prices of key raw materials begin to normalize, businesses anticipate that value growth will positively compound on top of the current volume growth in upcoming quarters [1].

Premiumisation and Market Penetration A significant tailwind is the ongoing trend of premiumisation, where consumers are increasingly opting for higher-quality or value-added offerings [2]. This is coupled with successful efforts to achieve a deeper reach across Tier II and Tier III cities and towns, allowing companies to capture new consumer bases while expanding their margins [2].

Digital Commerce and Multi-Channel Expansion The rapid adoption of modern sales channels is accelerating industry growth. Companies are seeing a strong growth trajectory in digital commerce, with some portfolios reporting digital channel growth as high as 43.4% year-over-year [3]. In addition to digital-first brand launches, there is steady traction across general trade, modern trade, quick-commerce, and export channels, ensuring products are highly accessible to diverse consumer segments [2, 3].

Strong Sector-Specific Demand Different product categories are also benefiting from unique, sector-specific tailwinds: * Specialty Fats and Butters: There is an increasing and robust demand for Cocoa Butter Equivalents (CBE) and other specialty fats and butters driven heavily by the chocolate, confectionery, and cosmetics sectors [4, 5]. * Nuts, Dry Fruits, and Ice Cream: Growth in this segment is being directly supported by GST rate rationalisation, which makes these products more accessible, alongside robust festive and wedding-season demand [2].

What is the general outlook of this industry?

asof: 2026-04-15

The general outlook across the various industry sectors mentioned—spanning sugar and refining, consumer goods (FMCG), and specialty fats—presents a mixed picture of global oversupply challenges in some areas and robust consumer demand in others.

Sugar and Refining Industry The global sugar market is expected to remain in a mild surplus right through the sugar year (SY) ’25-’26, with S&P Platts projecting a global surplus of 3.5 million metric tons (MMT) [1]. This surplus of refined and white sugar is accumulating at origins such as Brazil, Thailand, and India [2]. As a result, the industry faces several pricing pressures: * Declining Raw Sugar Prices: Prices have been trading lower due to lower hedge exposure by Brazilian and Thai millers, short positions built up by hedge funds, and a downward revision in raw demand from Indonesia [1, 3]. * Depressed White Premiums: Unfavourable global market conditions and excess refining capacity have eroded profitability [4]. White premium values are under pressure, continuing to trade in the range of USD 90 to USD 105 per MT, a trend expected to persist for at least the next two quarters [3, 5]. * Ethanol and Geopolitics: Weak oil prices are keeping ethanol parities at lower levels [3]. However, the current geopolitical outlook is very challenging, and any rally in oil prices driven by these risks could consequently impact sugar [3, 6].

In the Indian domestic sugar market, ISMA estimates for SY ’25-’26 project a gross production of 34.3 MMT, domestic consumption of 28.5 MMT, and a closing stock of about 6 MMT [7]. While states like Maharashtra, Karnataka, and Uttar Pradesh have seen a mild increase in crushing output, Tamil Nadu continues to lag [7, 8]. Indian millers are currently facing cost pressures because the Fair and Remunerative Price (FRP) for sugarcane regularly increases, while there has been no clarity on upward revisions to the Minimum Selling Price (MSP) or ethanol offtake prices, which have remained stagnant for almost three years [9, 10]. Furthermore, the Indian Meteorological Department has warned of El Nino signs that may impact rainfall distribution and reservoir levels later in the year [8].

FMCG and Consumer Foods (Spices, Nuts, Ice Cream, and Staples) The outlook for the consumer packaged goods and food sector shows an improving consumption environment [11]. Companies are experiencing sustained, robust demand driven by the festive and wedding seasons, premiumisation, and GST rate rationalisation [12]. There is also steady growth and deeper reach across Tier II and Tier III markets, supported by traction in general trade, quick-commerce, and export channels [12].

A significant dynamic in this sector is the continued deflation in key raw material prices [11]. For instance, the market price for pulses and lentils has been 35% to 40% lower compared to the previous year, causing a temporary drop in value turnovers despite steady volume growth [13]. However, as raw material prices begin to normalise in the coming quarters, value growth is expected to positively leverage on top of the expanding volume growth [11].

Specialty Fats and Butters For manufacturers of specialty fats, Cocoa Butter Equivalents (CBE), and exotic butters, there is robust demand in the chocolate, confectionery, and cosmetics sectors [14]. This strong demand environment is encouraging companies to undertake capital expenditure to increase their existing fractionation and manufacturing capacities to meet market needs [15].
