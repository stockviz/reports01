What are the headwinds affecting this industry?

asof: 2026-04-14

The artificial leather and footwear industry is currently navigating several significant headwinds, ranging from macroeconomic shifts to intense local and international competition:

Rising Raw Material Costs The industry is experiencing an upward trend in the cost of essential inputs. Specifically, the prices of plasticizers and yarn have started to increase, preventing any further softening of material costs [1, 2]. While companies often absorb minor fluctuations to avoid disturbing their customers, these rising input costs put ongoing pressure on maintaining strong profit margins [3, 4].

Influx of Cheap Chinese Imports Manufacturers are facing severe pressure from a surge of cheap imports originating from China [5, 6]. This influx is accompanied by market manipulation, which creates an uncertain demand scenario. Because of this unpredictable environment, companies are finding it difficult to confidently forecast outlooks or immediately reach optimal capacity utilization for new production lines, such as PU leather [5-8].

Intense Local Competition in the Footwear Segment The domestic footwear business is struggling to grow due to fierce local competition [9, 10]. This highly competitive environment has driven price margins down to very low levels, making the domestic footwear sector a primary area of concern for manufacturers [9, 10]. As a result, companies are deliberately shifting their focus away from these low-margin domestic segments and prioritizing high-margin export businesses instead [11, 12].

Degrowth in Key End-Markets For synthetic leather suppliers catering to Original Equipment Manufacturers (OEMs), sluggish end-markets present a major challenge. Notably, the European automotive industry—a major target market for premium artificial leather—is experiencing a period of degrowth rather than expansion [13, 14]. This forces suppliers to pivot their primary growth targets toward other regions, like the U.S. market, or focus more heavily on non-automotive sectors in Europe [13, 14].

Deglobalization and the Threat of Tariffs There is a growing macroeconomic shift towards “deglobalization,” which poses a long-term risk to export-heavy business models [15, 16]. While recent tariff impositions in regions like Mexico have not immediately impacted current operations, the looming threat of future trade barriers remains a serious consideration [17-20]. To counteract these protectionist trends, companies are being forced to evaluate highly capital-intensive countermeasures, such as setting up new manufacturing plants outside of India to sustain their global competitiveness [15, 16, 21, 22].

What are the key things to understand about this industry?

asof: 2026-04-14

The industry encompasses the manufacturing of artificial leather (PVC and PU synthetic leather), real leather, footwear, textile products, outerwear, and fashion apparels [1-5]. The applications for these materials are vast, covering automotive original equipment manufacturers (OEMs), auto replacements, marine applications, and home furnishing [1, 6, 7].

Global Clientele and Expansion Companies in this sector cater to major international brands and global retail clients [5, 8]. This includes supplying premium materials to automotive giants like Mercedes-Benz and BMW, as well as providing merchandising and apparel for global retailers such as PVH, Target, American Eagle, Marks & Spencer, Columbia Sportswear, and Primark [5, 8]. To better serve these international markets, companies are actively establishing subsidiary offices and stock points in regions like Europe and the United States [6, 9, 10].

Export-Driven Growth and Margins A defining characteristic of this industry is its aggressive focus on exports, particularly to the United States and European regions, which drives both top-line and bottom-line growth [6, 11, 12]. Export business is heavily prioritized because it yields significantly better profit margins compared to domestic sales [13, 14]. By concentrating on a premium export product mix, some companies are able to maintain robust EBITDA margins in the range of 24% to 25% [13]. While the domestic market is still targeted for steady growth, segments like domestic footwear face intense pressure from local unorganized competition and low-margin pricing, reinforcing the strategic pivot toward exports [14, 15].

Raw Material Sourcing and Cost Dynamics The production of synthetic leather and fashion goods relies heavily on raw materials such as PVC, plasticizers, and yarn [16]. A substantial portion of these raw materials—frequently estimated between one-third to over 60% of total material costs—must be imported from Europe, the US, and China [17, 18]. Consequently, companies are exposed to global raw material price fluctuations, though minor cost increases are usually absorbed by the manufacturers to avoid disturbing customer relationships, with price hikes only passed on during major market shifts [19, 20].

Currency and Pricing Mechanisms Export contracts and material prices are typically fixed in US dollars [21]. Because of this structure, the industry is highly sensitive to foreign exchange rates, and the depreciation of the Indian Rupee against currencies like the Dollar or Euro acts as a direct financial tailwind [21, 22]. This currency fluctuation frequently contributes to significant foreign exchange gains, which boost other income and overall profitability [22, 23].

Competition and Trade Strategies The industry faces significant ongoing challenges from cheap imports, particularly from China, which can heavily manipulate domestic market dynamics [24]. To counter broader geopolitical risks, deglobalization trends, and potential tariff impositions, companies are proactively evaluating the establishment of long-term manufacturing plants outside of India [25, 26]. Furthermore, international trade agreements, such as the anticipated EU-India Free Trade Agreement, are closely watched [27]. The potential elimination of duties through such agreements is expected to make Indian products much more price-competitive against global peers, providing a strong catalyst for both automotive and non-automotive business expansion [10].

What are the tailwinds affecting this industry?

asof: 2026-04-14

Free Trade Agreements (FTAs) A major tailwind for the Indian artificial leather and related products industry is the upcoming free trade agreement between the European Union (EU) and India [1, 2]. This agreement will effectively eliminate tariffs (zero duty) on Indian leather and related products entering the European market [1-4]. While it is expected to take approximately 10 to 12 months for the final papers to be signed by the EU and its member nations, the agreement is projected to strongly boost non-automotive business segments in Europe [3, 4].

Strong Export Market Demand There is increasing momentum in overseas markets, particularly within the US and European regions [5, 6]. Companies are actively securing lucrative export orders for Original Equipment Manufacturer (OEM) supplies, which is significantly contributing to their top-line and bottom-line growth [5, 6]. Management teams note that the growth in the export market is actively outpacing domestic market growth, and this strong export momentum is expected to continue over the next 2 to 3 years [5-8]. Additionally, specific niche segments, such as the marine business, are growing very fast in Europe [9, 10].

Global Expansion and Localization To fully capture these international opportunities and navigate a world that is shifting towards deglobalization, companies are actively expanding their physical footprints abroad [11, 12]. Businesses are setting up international subsidiaries—such as in Estonia—to hold local stock and tackle customer needs directly in the European market [9, 10, 13, 14]. Furthermore, companies are actively evaluating the establishment of manufacturing plants outside of India as a long-term countermeasure to potential future tariffs and to solidify their global presence [11, 12].

What is the general outlook of this industry?

asof: 2026-04-14

The general outlook for the synthetic leather, fashion apparel, and accessories industry is highly positive, characterized by robust revenue growth, strategic export expansion, and improving profitability. Companies in the fashion and apparel sector are experiencing strong momentum, with year-over-year revenue growth reaching up to 39%, driven by higher sales and improved market penetration [1]. Furthermore, higher capacity utilization and operating leverage have led to significant improvements in bottom-line metrics, such as a 390% growth in Profit Before Tax for certain fashion segments [1]. Market leaders in the artificial leather industry are similarly targeting double-digit volume and value growth, projecting an average 15% growth rate for the coming years [2-7].

Export-Led Expansion and Margin Improvement A major driver of this positive outlook is the industry’s concentrated focus on international markets, which consistently offer better profit margins than domestic sales [8-11]. Companies are securing significant export orders for OEM supply in the US and expect this strong momentum to continue for the next two to three years [12, 13]. To capitalize on overseas demand, manufacturers are establishing subsidiary companies in Europe and the UK to stock materials locally and directly serve regional customers in the automotive, marine, and furnishing aftermarket sectors [14-18].

The impending EU-India Free Trade Agreement is viewed as a substantial long-term tailwind; by effectively reducing import tariffs to zero, Indian manufacturers expect to become significantly more price-competitive against global peers, which is anticipated to strongly boost non-automotive business in Europe [19-24]. Due to this favorable shift towards a higher-margin export product mix, companies expect to comfortably sustain and potentially improve their EBITDA margins in the 24% to 25% range [8-11].

Domestic Market Challenges and Strategic Restructuring While the export outlook is exceptionally strong, the domestic market presents some headwinds due to intensifying local competition [25, 26]. Segments such as the domestic footwear replacement market are struggling to grow because of low-price margins and aggressive local competitors [25, 26]. In response, management teams are strategically pivoting away from low-margin domestic businesses, choosing instead to focus heavily on bottom-line profitability over pure top-line revenue growth and targeting premium domestic niches [9, 11, 27, 28]. To further enhance operational synergies and efficiency, some companies are actively restructuring by disposing of certain properties to generate additional liquidity and consolidating their plant and machinery into more optimized locations [29].

Raw Material Dynamics and Capex Future-Proofing The industry is navigating an environment of rising raw material costs, with the prices of inputs like PVC, plasticizers, and yarn trending upwards [30, 31]. However, the financial impact is mitigated by the fact that many major export contracts are fixed in dollars, providing a natural financial cushion when the rupee depreciates against foreign currencies [32-37]. Additionally, companies have demonstrated the ability to pass on significant raw material cost increases to both domestic and international customers when necessary, protecting their profit margins through well-indexed pricing models [33, 36, 38, 39].

To support future growth and hedge against the risks of global supply chain disruptions or potential foreign import tariffs, industry leaders are actively evaluating large-scale capital expenditures [40, 41]. This includes potential investments of ₹200 crores for new PVC manufacturing plants in South India, or up to ₹300 crores to establish manufacturing facilities outside of India on a global scale [42-45].
